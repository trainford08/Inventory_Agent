The six stories

Each team was designed to make a point about what real migrations look like — and to make sure every screen in the product has meaningful data behind it.

Alpha Platform
“The easy yes”
On track

Small, modern team. One clean codebase, a standard build pipeline, nothing weird under the hood. The system did most of the work automatically — the team lead just has to glance through and approve.

Team lead
Alex Chen, Engineering Lead
Team size
3 engineers
Codebase
TypeScript, ~2 GB
When they'll cut over
About 6 weeks from now

What makes them interesting

  • Almost nothing to review — proves the system can stay out of the way when things are simple.
  • High confidence on how much work is left (4–6 weeks of effort).
  • Comfortable buffer — no time pressure.
Role in the demo: the fast path. Shows what migration feels like when everything cooperates.
Bravo Services
“The typical healthy team”
On track

Normal team doing normal work. Eight repositories, a couple of older release pipelines that need a decision, a few third-party add-ons that don't have direct equivalents on the new platform. Nothing is blocking, but there are real choices to make.

Team lead
Jordan Kim, Staff Engineer
Team size
6 engineers
Activity
~58 active work items
When they'll cut over
About 10 weeks from now

What makes them interesting

  • The median experience — most real teams will look something like this.
  • A few judgment calls but no emergencies.
  • Demonstrates the steady, day-to-day review pace.
Role in the demo: the baseline. What "typical" looks like for the bulk of the fleet.
Charlie Toolkit
“The interesting one”
At risk

A mid-size team with one foot in modern software and one in legacy tooling. Old-style pipelines, an on-prem signing service that doesn't exist on the target platform, and a FedRAMP High security classification that creates strict rules about where data can live. This team has the most real migration decisions of any in the demo.

Team lead
Rory McDonald, Principal Engineer
Team size
11 engineers
Security
FedRAMP High (regulated)
When they'll cut over
~14 weeks — already 2 weeks behind

What makes them interesting

  • The federal compliance requirements force specific architectural decisions.
  • The on-prem signing service has no direct equivalent — someone has to figure out a replacement.
  • Already running 2 weeks behind schedule — the kind of team a program lead actually has to pay attention to.
  • Every "interesting" review state in the product shows up here: anomalies, needs-more-info flags, low-confidence evidence.
Role in the demo: the hero team. The richest review experience — this is the team you'd actually walk through during the pitch.
Delta Data Platform
“The heavyweight”
On track

Large data platform, mid-migration. Almost 100 GB of code. A separate 38 GB data repository with its own special handling. Shared code that pulls in from other teams' projects. Canary deployments coordinated across four regions. The big decisions are already made — what's left is executing without breaking anything.

Team lead
Elena Volkov, Engineering Director
Team size
14 engineers
Security
FedRAMP High (regulated)
When they'll cut over
About 8 weeks from now — zero buffer

What makes them interesting

  • The "monster repo" case — tests how the product handles very large codebases.
  • Coordination across regions means any slip cascades.
  • Already 20 weeks into the work — execution risk, not decision risk.
  • No slack left: a single delay pushes the cutover date.
Role in the demo: the scale case. Proves the product handles teams where a misstep costs weeks.
Echo Mobile
“The Champion still has work”
Blocked

iOS + Android team, mid-review. The system did its part — discovery turned up a bunch of questions about signing keys, the mobile build service, and mobile-specific release gates. The team lead just hasn't answered most of them yet. Everything downstream is stuck until they do.

Team lead
Karen Lee, Mobile Lead
Team size
4 engineers
Status
Blocked on Champion input
When they'll cut over
Can't be estimated yet

What makes them interesting

  • Shows what happens when the human side of the process stalls.
  • No effort estimate, no target date — all downstream of answers the team lead owes.
  • Mobile-specific complications (signing, app stores) that most teams don't have.
Role in the demo: the stuck case. The team that makes the "needs your attention" queue on the program dashboard light up.

Why six, and why these six?

Real migration programs have hundreds or thousands of teams, and every one is different. The demo's six are curated so that together they cover the whole range:

  • Every health state — On track, At risk, Blocked, and Done are all represented.
  • Every stage of the journey — from "haven't started" through "mid-cutover" to "rolled back."
  • Every kind of difficulty — easy, typical, regulated, huge, stalled, and paused.

That way, every screen in the product has real data to show — and every story a champion, a team lead, or a program PM might tell about their teams has a cohort that matches.
